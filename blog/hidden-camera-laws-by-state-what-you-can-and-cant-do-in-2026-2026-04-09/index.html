The Current State of Hidden Camera Laws in 2026

Hidden camera legislation has become increasingly complex as technology outpaces legal frameworks. What's perfectly legal in one state can result in serious criminal charges in another. For anyone interested in security cameras, home surveillance, or concerned about privacy violations, understanding these distinctions is essential.

The landscape of surveillance law by state reflects a patchwork of regulations influenced by state constitutions, court decisions, and evolving privacy concerns. No single federal law governs hidden cameras uniformly across the United States, meaning individuals and businesses must navigate different legal requirements depending on their location.

Two-Party Consent vs. One-Party Consent States

The most critical distinction in hidden camera state laws centers on audio recording consent. Video recording typically faces fewer restrictions than audio, but this varies significantly.

Two-Party Consent States

In two-party consent states—including California, Florida, Illinois, Maryland, Massachusetts, Michigan, Montana, New Hampshire, Pennsylvania, and Washington—all parties involved in a conversation must consent to recording. This applies to both video with audio and audio-only recording.

  • Recording a conversation without explicit consent is a felony in many of these states
  • Penalties can include fines up to $10,000 and imprisonment up to 15 years
  • This applies even if you're a participant in the conversation
  • Home security systems recording family members without consent may violate these laws

One-Party Consent States

The remaining states operate under one-party consent, meaning only one participant needs to agree to recording. This is considerably more permissive for surveillance purposes.

  • You can legally record conversations you're part of without others' knowledge
  • Restrictions still apply to recording in areas with heightened privacy expectations (bathrooms, bedrooms)
  • Business recording policies still require reasonable notice in many cases

Video Recording in Private Spaces

Video recording faces fewer legal obstacles than audio, but expectations of privacy remain paramount across all states. The fundamental rule: people have a reasonable expectation of privacy in bathrooms, bedrooms, dressing rooms, and showers.

Legal in Most States

  • Home security cameras in common areas (living rooms, hallways, kitchens)
  • Business surveillance in public-facing areas
  • Nanny cameras in shared household spaces
  • Workplace monitoring in non-sensitive areas

Illegal in All States

  • Hidden cameras in bathrooms or locker rooms
  • Recording through windows into private residences
  • Cameras in hotel rooms or rental properties without disclosure
  • Recording in areas where subjects have reasonable privacy expectations

Business and Workplace Considerations

Employers have broader recording rights than individuals, but even business surveillance faces restrictions. Most states require employers to provide notice that recording is occurring, though the specificity of this notice varies.

States like California impose stricter requirements, while others permit workplace monitoring with minimal disclosure. Audio recording of employee conversations without consent remains problematic in two-party consent states regardless of employer status.

Tenant and Landlord Privacy Rights

Rental properties occupy a gray area in surveillance law by state. Landlords typically cannot install hidden cameras in tenant spaces, even in common areas, without explicit consent and notice. Some states like New York and Vermont have specific statutes prohibiting landlord recording of tenants without clear disclosure.

Practical Recommendation for 2026

Before installing any surveillance system, research your specific state's laws or consult a local attorney. The safest approach involves visible cameras with proper notice to anyone being recorded. If audio recording is necessary, verify your state's consent requirements first. When in doubt, obtain explicit written consent from all parties who might be recorded.

The hidden camera landscape continues evolving, but the underlying principle remains consistent: privacy rights take precedence over surveillance interests across American law. Respecting these boundaries protects you legally while maintaining ethical surveillance practices.
