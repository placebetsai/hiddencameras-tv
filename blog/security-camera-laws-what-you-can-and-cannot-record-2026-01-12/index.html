Security Camera Laws: What You Can and Cannot Record

Security cameras have become standard in homes and businesses, offering peace of mind and protection against theft and vandalism. However, many property owners don't realize that recording video is heavily regulated by federal, state, and local laws. Installing a camera without understanding these legal boundaries can result in civil lawsuits, criminal charges, and significant fines. This guide clarifies what you can and cannot legally record with security cameras.

Federal Laws and Two-Party Consent Rules

The primary federal law governing surveillance is the Electronic Communications Privacy Act (ECPA), which prohibits the intentional interception of wire, oral, or electronic communications. When it comes to audio recording, this is where most homeowners run into legal trouble. Federal law requires all-party consent for audio recording in many situations, meaning everyone being recorded must knowingly agree.

Video recording is generally less regulated than audio, but the rules still apply. You can record video on your property without consent, but audio recording is a different story. Many states follow the all-party consent rule, while others use a one-party consent standard. Understanding your state's specific laws is critical before activating microphone features on your security cameras.

What You Can Legally Record

On your own property, you have broad rights to install security cameras:

  • Entrances and exits: Front doors, back doors, and garage entrances are ideal recording locations
  • Common areas: Living rooms, kitchens, and hallways can be monitored in residential properties
  • Outdoor spaces: Driveways, patios, and yards are generally safe to record
  • Business areas: Commercial properties can record employee work areas with proper notification

In these locations, you can record video 24/7 without legal consequences, provided you follow state-specific audio recording laws. The key requirement is that the camera must be installed on property you own or control. Placing cameras to record your neighbor's yard, even partially, violates their privacy rights and can expose you to liability.

Prohibited Recording Areas and Situations

Never install security cameras in areas where people have a reasonable expectation of privacy. These include:

  • Bathrooms and changing rooms
  • Bedrooms (both in residential and rental properties)
  • Private offices with closed doors
  • Areas explicitly designated as private spaces in rental agreements
  • Neighbor's properties without consent

Violating these restrictions can result in criminal charges for voyeurism or invasion of privacy, regardless of whether audio is captured. Additionally, recording into neighboring properties without explicit consent is illegal in all states and can lead to both civil and criminal liability.

State-Specific Audio Recording Laws

Audio recording regulations vary significantly by state. All-party consent states require everyone in a conversation to agree to recording. These include California, Florida, Illinois, Maryland, Massachusetts, Michigan, Montana, New Hampshire, Pennsylvania, and Washington. Recording audio without consent in these states is a felony in some cases.

One-party consent states allow one participant to record a conversation without notifying others. This applies to most other states, though some have nuanced exceptions.

Before purchasing a camera with microphone capabilities, verify your state's audio recording laws. Many security camera manufacturers disable audio recording in all-party consent states by default.

Notification Requirements for Employees and Tenants

If you operate a business or manage rental properties, additional rules apply. Employers must notify employees that surveillance is occurring in workplace areas. Failing to do so can violate state wiretapping laws and employee privacy rights. Post visible signs indicating camera surveillance in common areas.

For rental properties, landlords must inform tenants about security cameras before they move in. Most states require this disclosure in the lease agreement. Hidden cameras in rental properties are illegal, even in areas like living rooms where surveillance is otherwise permitted.

Key Takeaway

Security cameras are valuable tools for protecting your property, but legal compliance is non-negotiable. Research your state's specific laws regarding both video and audio recording, install cameras only in appropriate locations, and provide proper notification to employees and tenants. When in doubt, consult with a local attorney to ensure your security system complies with all applicable regulations. This proactive approach protects both your legal standing and others' privacy rights.
